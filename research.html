  • AI Adoption and Firm-Level Inequality: A Heterogeneous-Firm Model

    Working Paper · April 2026 · JEL D43, L11, L13, O33

    A heterogeneous-firm model in the Melitz tradition where AI’s productivity gains rise in baseline firm productivity. Proves four results: industry concentration rises, the entry cutoff rises (cheap AI does not lower entry barriers), welfare decomposes into productivity, variety, and markup channels, and between-firm wage dispersion grows. Access mandates dominate subsidies on welfare and entry margins.

  • Tacit Collusion Through Shared LLM Pricing Tools

    Working Paper · April 2026 · JEL L13, L41, K21, D43

    A one-shot coordination model in which a shared LLM acts as a de facto correlation device for competing firms’ pricing decisions. The mechanism is the training-induced prior conditional on a shared prompt. This reframes tacit collusion as a technology-adoption problem and gives antitrust practitioners concrete prompt-level features to regulate.

  • Cultural Homogenization Through Common AI: A Lemons Model with Endogenous Detection

    Working Paper · April 2026

    An Akerlof-style market for distinctiveness with noisy detection. Endogenizing detection investment yields multiple equilibria and tipping dynamics, so cultural homogenization is not self-limiting. Disclosure mandates welfare-dominate in the unraveling region; private curation can be preferred when detection is cheap.

  • AI Companionship and the Market for Thin Relationships

    Working Paper · April 2026

    AI substitutes for the thin ties of casual acquaintance, not for thick ties, and not for either tier’s role in building relational skill. Five propositions characterize a corner-style reallocation, a long-run skill decline, and a regressive distribution of harm. Closed-form crossing times under CES–Cobb–Douglas; a parallel framework gives the “juniority paradox” for cognitive labor.

  • AI and the Economics of Loneliness: A Framework for Regime-Aware Welfare Analysis

    Working Paper · 2026 · JEL D11, D91, I12, I31, L86

    Resolves the apparent contradiction between short-run trials (AI relieves loneliness) and longer-run observational studies (AI entrenches it) by treating loneliness as a two-regime dynamical system. Calibrated against Fang et al. (2025), reproduces the null ATE alongside large heterogeneous effects and partitions a vulnerable sub-population current AI products do not observe.

  • Vickrey-Style RFQs for Event-Contract Exchanges

    Working Paper · April 2026 · Mechanism Design

    A small, targeted change to Kalshi’s RFQ-to-CLOB workflow: replace pay-as-bid with a tick-aware second-price rule based on the best competing quote. Preserves the documented privacy model, feasibility checks, and timing while removing the main incentive to strategically shade quotes. A clear, testable hypothesis for event-contract markets.

  • Proxy-Reward Indistinguishability and Audit Recovery in POMDPs

    Working Paper · April 2026 · AI Alignment

    A non-identifiability theorem for partially observed environments: under a local degeneracy condition, honest and reward-hacking policies can have identical proxy-reward law while their true returns differ by a quantitative gap. A privileged audit channel restores distinguishability with matching upper and lower bounds on audited episodes.

  • Defaults in Small Business Loans: Prediction Models and Implicit Bias

    With Shrihari Sridhar · AMA Educators Winter Conference 2023

    Using SBA loan data from 1968–2014 (865k loans, 33k ZIP codes) merged with Census demographics, asks whether ZIP-code data improves default prediction at the cost of inducing implicit bias. Logistic and neural-net specifications show a 17× improvement in classification accuracy alongside a measurable bias toward smaller, whiter, higher-income ZIPs.
